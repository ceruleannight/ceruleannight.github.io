Assets
Anything that a company owns that holds quantifiable monetary value.

Liabilities
All debts and legal obligations of a company that must be paid over time.

Equity
There are various definitions depending on the exact context, but at a basic level Equity can be defined as Assets minus Liabilities.
See: Equity

Book Value
Book Value as a concept is very closely related to Equity. Basically it is the amount that would be paid to share holders if a company were immediately liquidated. That is if the assets were sold for value and all liabilities paid off and the rest distributed to the shareholders.
See: Book Value

EV: Enterprise Value
This is effectively the cost of taking over a company. It is basically the market cap plus debts minus cash. Some people prefer to use this value in place of market cap or price in various metrics as it better reflects the impact to your equity by owning a company.
See: Enterprise Value

P/E or PE
Price divided by Earnings. This is used when evaluating whether or not a company is at a fair price. There are several PE types. Usually PE refers to the current stock price divided by the earnings for the trailing twelve months. This is sometimes qualified as PE TTM, or Trailing PE. Forward PE refers to the current price divided by analyst projected earning, usually for the next twelve months. For many industries a PE of 15 is considered a fair price.
Cyclically-adjusted P/E or CAPE or PE10 or Shiller PE refer to an alternate way of assessing PE that divides the current price by an average the earnings adjusted for inflation over a ten year period. Some feel that this give a better relative picture of valuation.

PEG
P/E divided by analyst projected annual growth as a percent. This is used when evaluating whether or not a company is at a fair price. This is considered a better metric to use than PE by some because it factors in projected future growth of the company. A generally accepted rule of thumb is that a PEG of one or less is a sign of fair value and a value greater one is considered overpriced.

EBITDA

TTM - Trailing Twelve Months
MRQ - Most Recent Quarter

YOY
Year over year. Prospective investors usually evaluate a company's quarterly earnings relative to the same quarter from the year earlier. This is called year-over-year comparison. This is done because so many industries and companies have earnings that are cyclical on an annual basis.

10K
A company's annual report that they must file with the SEC.
